### Half-Page Summary

ATLAS is a structured, multi-dimensional framework for evaluating the epistemic legitimacy of theories, explanatory frameworks, and claims about reality. It was created to solve a concrete and consequential problem: the word "theory" is used so inconsistently — ranging from a casual guess to the most robust form of scientific knowledge — that public discourse, education, policy, and research all suffer from the resulting confusion. Ideas that have been decisively falsified are discussed as "competing with" theories backed by overwhelming convergent evidence. Simultaneously, rock-solid scientific knowledge is dismissed as "just a theory," implying it is no more reliable than opinion.

ATLAS brings systematic clarity to this spectrum. It evaluates any theory across three independent dimensions: **truth status** (Has this been proven, disproven, or is it unresolved?), **scientific engagement** (Does it participate honestly in scientific testing and revision?), and **explanatory integration** (Does it fit coherently within the broader body of established knowledge, or does it fracture it?). Each dimension is scored on its own scale, and the three scores are combined into a composite ATLAS score ranging from −5 to +10.

Crucially, the framework is built on a strict hierarchy: truth status is primary. No amount of methodological elegance or integrative appeal can rescue a debunked claim. No narrow domain scope can undermine a theory that has achieved mechanistic closure. Two override rules — the Negative Override Rule and the Closure Override Rule — enforce this hierarchy mathematically.

Beyond individual theory evaluation, ATLAS is designed to grow into a public database of evaluated theories. Once a sufficient body of evaluations exists, the relationships between theories can be mapped, producing a "Tree of Knowledge" — a visual and structural map of where scientific understanding is settled, where it is actively developing, and where it remains genuinely open.

The framework is grounded in a specific philosophical position called *Non-Reductionist Representational Realism*, which affirms that objective knowledge is attainable, that evidential closure is real, and that some claims can be definitively known to be true or false — explicitly rejecting both fallibilism (the view that no claim can ever be settled) and the misrepresentation of science as "scientism" (the strawman claim that science is dogmatic or religion-like).

**Who this is for:** Educators, researchers, science communicators, policy makers, AI developers building epistemic reasoning systems, and anyone working to distinguish reliable knowledge from speculation in a high-noise information environment.[^1]

## Core Thesis

The central argument is this: **the word "theory" covers a vast spectrum of epistemic standing, and the absence of a clear, structured standard for evaluating that spectrum causes systematic damage to knowledge culture.** Without such a standard, settled science is equated with speculation, debunked claims are treated as "one perspective," and public reasoning collapses into false equivalence.

ATLAS is the proposed solution: a transparent, multi-axis scoring framework that evaluates theories on truth status (primary), scientific engagement (secondary), and explanatory integration (secondary). It produces a composite score and a three-part profile for any theory, making the spectrum of knowledge visible and navigable.[^1]

The thesis is not merely descriptive — it carries a normative claim: *some things are proven, some things are debunked, and a functioning knowledge culture must be able to say so clearly and without apology.*[^1]

---

## Major Themes

### Theme 1: The Spectrum of Knowledge Must Be Made Visible

Scientific knowledge is not binary (known vs. unknown). It exists along a spectrum: from firmly established and mechanistically understood, to strongly supported but still refining, to developing with promising evidence, to speculative but testable in principle, to falsified by convergent evidence. ATLAS's foundational premise is that this spectrum is real, important, and currently invisible in public discourse. Without a standard way to place theories on this spectrum, every claim gets treated as equivalent — which is both intellectually dishonest and practically dangerous.[^1]

### Theme 2: Truth Status Is Primary — Nothing Else Can Override It

The most structurally important theme in the document is the strict hierarchy of epistemic evaluation. Truth status — whether a theory has been confirmed, is unresolved, or has been debunked — is the primary and decisive dimension. A theory's methodological rigor (how it participates in science) and its conceptual elegance (how well it integrates across disciplines) are real and important, but they are secondary and diagnostic. They cannot rescue a debunked claim, and they cannot undermine a proven one. This hierarchy is enforced architecturally through two scoring override rules.[^1]

### Theme 3: Category Collapse Is the Root Epistemic Error

Much confusion in public discourse arises from conflating distinct evaluative categories. Truth is confused with popularity. Correctness is confused with methodological participation. Scientific authority is confused with dogma. ATLAS is designed as a direct antidote to category collapse — it separates evaluative dimensions explicitly so that strength in one cannot be mistaken for strength in another.[^1]

### Theme 4: Epistemic Closure Is Real — Fallibilism Is a Problem

A running counter-argument throughout the document is the rejection of fallibilism: the philosophical position that no knowledge can ever be considered settled. ATLAS argues that fallibilism, while beginning from the valid observation that human knowledge is not omniscient, makes an invalid move — it treats the absence of *absolute* certainty as evidence that *stable, justified* knowledge cannot be established. This is a category error. ATLAS affirms that objective knowledge is attainable, evidential closure is real, and claims like "water is H₂O" or "the Earth is not flat" are not provisional opinions — they are proven facts within their domains.[^1]

### Theme 5: Scale-Relative Epistemology

A major conceptual innovation is the introduction of ontological scale as a necessary variable in epistemic evaluation. Reality is stratified — from quantum field structures at the Nano scale to cosmological structures at the Meta scale — and a theory's explanatory closure must always be evaluated relative to the specific ontological band it operates within. Refinement at a lower scale cannot destabilize closure achieved at a higher scale unless actual contradiction appears at that same level.[^1]

### Theme 6: Science Is Not Scientism

The document devotes substantial space to rejecting the misrepresentation of science as "scientism" — a rhetorical move that frames science as dogmatic religion. ATLAS argues that this is a straw man: science is defined by falsifiability, peer review, and reproducibility — the exact opposite of dogma. When critics label science as ideologically rigid, they are targeting a constructed version of science that does not reflect how it actually operates. Once this misrepresentation is removed, the gap between validated knowledge and unsupported claims cannot be obscured by appeals to "equal perspectives."[^1]

### Theme 7: ATLAS as Infrastructure for a Knowledge Database

Beyond individual theory evaluation, ATLAS is designed to function as the architecture for a growing public database. Once sufficiently populated, the database enables a second layer of insight: mapping the relationships between theories across disciplines, visualizing where knowledge is settled versus developing, and producing what the document calls a "Tree of Knowledge" — a meta-level structure of scientific understanding.[^1]

### Chapter 1: Introduction — The Problem of Theory Evaluation

**Key argument:** The word "theory" is used inconsistently across ordinary language and scientific discourse, creating a systemic epistemic confusion. In everyday speech, a theory can mean a guess or a speculation. In science, it can mean either a new idea under investigation *or* the most reliable form of knowledge we have. There is currently no consistent standard for distinguishing between these levels.

**Problems this causes:**

- False equivalence: speculative ideas are placed alongside established knowledge as if comparable
- Public misunderstanding: people confuse science's openness to refinement with permanent uncertainty
- Downgrading: well-confirmed theories are dismissed as "just theories" (implying mere opinion)
- Communication distortion: communicators either overstate certainty (sounding like propaganda) or understate it (implying everything is unresolved)

**ATLAS's stated purpose:** To bring clarity to this problem by evaluating where a theory stands in relation to evidence, scientific inquiry, and the broader knowledge system. It does not judge a theory's cultural significance, philosophical interest, or moral appeal — only its epistemic status.

**The three independent dimensions:**

1. Truth status (ETS) — Has this been confirmed, is it unresolved, or has it been debunked?
2. Scientific engagement (SES) — Does it participate in scientific testing and revision?
3. Explanatory integration (EIS) — Does it cohere with established knowledge, or fracture it?

**The database vision:** ATLAS is designed to function not just as a one-off evaluation tool but as a growing reference database. Once populated, it enables mapping of relationships between theories across disciplines, revealing where knowledge converges, where tensions remain, and where understanding is still developing.[^1]

### Chapter 2: The ATLAS Framework Overview

**Core structure:** ATLAS evaluates theories using three distinct scales and produces two outputs: (1) a three-part profile (ETS + SES + EIS), and (2) a unified composite score.

**Structural rule:** ETS is primary. SES and EIS provide diagnostic context. Evaluation always begins with ETS.

**Why the scales are separate:**

- A theory can behave scientifically and still be wrong
- A theory can be unresolved yet methodologically responsible
- A theory can integrate elegantly yet lack sufficient evidence
- A theory can be debunked yet retain cultural influence

**Scale ranges:**

- ETS: −1 to +4
- SES: −2 to +3
- EIS: −2 to +3
- Composite: −5 to +10

**The three conditions ATLAS operates across:**

1. Debunked theories — secondary scales cannot restore legitimacy
2. Established theories — secondary scales do not diminish confirmed truth
3. Unresolved theories — secondary scales provide critical diagnostic insight[^1]

### Chapter 3: Conceptual Foundations of ATLAS

**What counts as a theory?** ATLAS uses "theory" in its scientific sense: a structured explanatory framework designed to explain a phenomenon or class of phenomena and to be accountable to evidence and rational evaluation. It is NOT a guess, a worldview, a preference, or a loose narrative.

**Related but distinct categories:**

- **Hypothesis:** A specific testable claim; a theory contains many hypotheses
- **Data:** Observations and measurements; data does not explain itself — a theory interprets data
- **Worldview:** A broad interpretive posture about meaning and values; not testable as a single unit

**Boundary conditions for scientific evaluation:** A claim qualifies for evaluation when it:

- Makes testable or falsifiable predictions
- Proposes causal explanations examinable through investigation
- Competes with established frameworks and can be evaluated against them
- Produces measurable effects that can be independently verified

**Falsifiability:** Central criterion. A claim must be vulnerable to disconfirmation. Some claims become falsifiable by conflicting with well-established frameworks (e.g., creationist accounts are falsified by the convergent evidence for evolution, even without direct testing of creationism itself).

**Verifiability:** Phenomena must be observable in ways permitting independent verification and systematic study. Anecdotal reports and unrepeatable observations do not meet this threshold.

**Speculative possibilities vs. evaluated claims:** ATLAS distinguishes:

- Investigable possibilities awaiting evidence (e.g., extraterrestrial life)
- Unsupported assertions lacking measurable support
- Falsified claims contradicted by established knowledge

**Burden of proof:** Rests on the party making the claim. "What can be asserted without evidence can be dismissed without evidence." This does not establish that the claim is false — only that it has not met the threshold for serious scientific consideration.

**Inclusion/exclusion boundaries:** ATLAS evaluates claims that function as explanatory competitors within scientific or philosophical inquiry — including creationism or idealist doctrines when presented as competing explanations. It does not evaluate claims about ghosts, psychic powers, or miraculous healings unless they produce repeatable, independently verifiable evidence.

**Multi-axis epistemic legitimacy:** Collapsing evaluative domains produces persistent confusion. ATLAS separates them intentionally:

- ETS: Is the theory true, unresolved, or debunked?
- SES: Does it participate in scientific accountability?
- EIS: Does it cohere with established knowledge?

**Why ATLAS excludes certain domains from scoring:**

- Harm assessment (depends on context, values, time horizon — not a truth detector)
- Cultural impact (reflects influence, not evidence)
- Practical utility (something can work while partly wrong)
- Institutional consensus (authority does not establish truth — ATLAS evaluates directly)[^1]

### Chapter 4: Ontological Scale and Epistemic Stability

This is one of the most philosophically rich chapters. It introduces the concept of **ontological scale** as necessary for properly understanding epistemic closure.

**Key definitions:**

- **Ontology:** What exists; the structure of reality itself
- **Epistemology:** How we know; methods of investigation, justification, and explanation
- **Phenomenon:** Something that occurs in reality, independent of our theories about it

**The Five Ontological Scales:**


| Scale            | Level                                     | Sublevels / Examples                                                                                                                                      |
| ---------------- | ----------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Nano**         | Smallest constitutive structures          | Planck/quantum field structures; subatomic particles (quarks, leptons, bosons); atomic structures (protons, neutrons, electrons)                          |
| **Micro**        | Bounded physical systems of Nano elements | Molecular structures; biochemical/cellular structures; organismic structures (multicellular organisms)                                                    |
| **Intermediate** | Planetary-contained systems               | Ecological systems; planetary physical systems (climate, tectonic, oceanic); evolutionary processes; social/institutional systems; civilizational systems |
| **Macro**        | Extra-planetary astrophysical structures  | Planetary systems (solar systems); stellar and galactic structures; large astrophysical formations (galaxy clusters)                                      |
| **Meta**         | Universe-level structure                  | Cosmological structure; cosmic origin models (inflationary expansion); boundary condition questions; multiverse hypotheses                                |


**Holonic containment:** Each scale is a holon — Nano structures are contained within Micro systems, Micro within Intermediate, and so on. Containment does not imply reduction. A higher scale does not invalidate a lower scale. A lower scale does not dissolve the coherence of a higher one.

**Epistemic Closure — Four Types:**

1. **Phenomenon Closure:** The existence of a phenomenon has been decisively established within its ontological scale. The question of *whether* it exists is resolved. Investigation shifts from *whether* to *how*. (Examples: the existence of gravity, cellular division, cosmic expansion)
2. **Mechanistic Closure:** The causal processes responsible for producing a phenomenon have been sufficiently mapped to generate reliable prediction. Not just that something exists, but *why* it operates as it does. Domain-relative: mechanistic closure is always bounded by the scope of the domain. (Examples: DNA double helix structure explains genetic replication)
3. **Domain Closure:** Explanatory completion within a clearly delimited scope — both phenomenon and mechanistic stability have been achieved within the defined domain. Adjacent domains may remain open.
4. **Scale Level Closure:** The fundamental organizing processes governing an entire ontological scale are secure. (Examples: gravity as a governing interaction across astrophysical systems; thermodynamic constraint across physical systems; evolutionary transformation across biological systems)

**The Data Expansion Layer:** Epistemic closure does not terminate inquiry. After closure is achieved, data expansion continues — the ongoing refinement, extension, and resolution increase within a stabilized framework. New species discovered do not reopen the question of whether evolutionary transformation governs biology. Additional galactic mapping does not destabilize cosmological structure. Data expansion operates *within* closure, not against it.

**Epistemic Plateau — Three Types:**

1. **Technical plateau:** Tool-based constraints (instrumentation limits, computational capacity, measurement precision) — historically common and often temporary
2. **Structural plateau:** Physics-based constraints imposed by reality itself (signal speed limits, quantum measurement boundaries, thermodynamic irreversibility, cosmological horizon limits) — potentially permanent
3. **Observational plateau:** Access-based constraints (phenomena beyond direct or indirect reach due to scale, distance, or irreversible information loss)

**Six Principles of Epistemic Stability:**

1. **Scale Relativity of Closure:** Closure is always relative to ontological scale. Specifying scale does not weaken validity — it clarifies the conditions under which a claim is true.
2. **The Same-Scale Revision Rule:** Closure at a given scale can only be overturned by contradiction operating at *that same scale*. Nano-scale refinement does not dissolve Macro-scale structure.
3. **The Plateau Non-Negation Principle:** Investigative limits do not imply explanatory incompleteness. Plateau defines the horizon of access; closure defines completeness of explanation.
4. **The Refinement-Closure Distinction:** Ongoing refinement and new instances do not reopen settled explanatory structure unless same-scale contradiction emerges.
5. **The Non-Conflation Rule:** Ontological scale and epistemic status must not be confused. Lower-scale discovery must not be mistaken for higher-scale debunking.
6. **The Mechanistic Maturity Principle:** Domains can reach mechanistic maturity — the governing dynamics are sufficiently understood that most newly discovered phenomena can be interpreted as variations, configurations, or applications of already known mechanisms.

**Civilizational Knowledge Across Scale:** The document introduces a conceptual diagram distinguishing:

- **Absolute Knowledge:** Total set of all truths about reality (the "territory")
- **Potential civilizational epistemic plateau:** The range of knowledge a sufficiently advanced scientific civilization could plausibly achieve given cognitive and physical constraints
- **Current civilizational knowledge:** What humanity has actually developed so far

Key claim: humanity has likely achieved **ontological scale closure** — we have identified all major bands of phenomena across which physical reality operates (from quantum to cosmological). What remains is not the discovery of entirely new ontological layers but continued exploration *within* known scales.

**Scale Breadth vs. Depth:**

- Breadth perspective: Humanity knows almost nothing — the portion of the universe investigated is extraordinarily small
- Depth perspective: Humanity possesses conceptual frameworks for all major scales of phenomena
- Both are true simultaneously — scientific knowledge can be deep across scale while remaining thin across breadth[^1]

### Chapter 5: Non-Reductionist Representational Realism and the Rejection of Fallibilism and Scientism

**Philosophical foundation:** ATLAS operates from the position of **Non-Reductionist Representational Realism:**

- Reality exists independently of our descriptions
- Knowledge consists of structured representations of that reality
- These representations are not identical to reality itself ("the map is not the territory")
- But a good map *corresponds* to the territory — and when correspondence is achieved through precise definitions and convergent evidence, it constitutes *objective truth*

**Three levels of truth:**

- **Absolute truth:** The actual reality of a thing as it exists, independent of description — what Kant called the *Ding an sich* (thing-in-itself). This is the territory.
- **Objective truth:** Claims that accurately map the territory using clearly defined symbols and reproducible methods. Expressed through relative language, but refers to real features of the world.
- **Subjective truth:** What a person knows with certainty about their own experience (pain, sadness). Real, but not externally testable in the same way.

**The map analogy:** Scientific understanding is like a partially completed puzzle. Even with missing pieces, the overall image can be clearly visible, and the visible structure constrains what pieces can fit. Science is in this position: knowledge is not total, but convergent evidence has established stable large-scale structure, consistent patterns, and reliable mechanisms.

**Why fallibilism is a problem:**

- Fallibilism begins from the valid observation that human knowledge is not omniscient
- It then makes an invalid move: treating the absence of *absolute* certainty as evidence that *stable, justified* knowledge cannot exist
- This is a category error — it conflates absolute reality with objective knowledge
- It collapses the distinction between "we cannot know everything" and "we cannot know anything"
- It is self-contradictory: the assertion "we cannot truly know anything" is itself presented as a claim to certainty
- It removes the foundation that makes language meaningful — if nothing can be known, there is no way to distinguish coherence from nonsense
- It dissolves the conditions required for communication, reasoning, and any serious claim, including its own

**Why "scientism" is a misrepresentation:**

- Science is not a religion or doctrine — it is defined by falsifiability, peer review, and reproducibility, which are the exact opposite of dogma
- Scientific consensus is not enforced belief — it is the result of repeated testing and elimination of competing explanations that fail
- Science does not restrict reality to externally measurable objects — it requires claims about *any* phenomenon to be structured in a way that allows testing and evaluation
- Subjective phenomena (thoughts, emotions, conscious experience) are studied scientifically through structured reporting, comparison, and correlation — not dismissed
- Intersubjective phenomena (language, culture, shared meaning) are studied systematically through sociology, linguistics, and related fields
- The defining feature of science is not the *type* of data, but the *structure of evaluation* applied to it

**Epistemic humility vs. discriminating wisdom:**

- Epistemic humility: knowledge can be refined and models improved
- Discriminating wisdom: evidence can converge decisively, closure can be achieved, some claims are settled while others remain open
- These are not in tension — they describe different aspects of how knowledge works[^1]

### Chapters 6–9: The Three ATLAS Scales in Detail

#### The Established Truth Scale (ETS) — Primary Scale

**What it measures:** The truth status of a theory. Has this theory been confirmed as accurately describing reality? Does it accurately map the territory?

**Central question:** *Is this theory established, unresolved, or debunked?*

**Evaluation criteria:**

- Convergence of evidence across independent lines of investigation
- Predictive reliability — does it reliably forecast outcomes?
- Reproducibility — can findings be independently replicated?
- Stability across independent investigation — does it hold up regardless of who tests it?
- Resilience under falsification attempts — has it survived sustained efforts to disprove it?

**ETS Scale Levels:**


| Score  | Label                       | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                       |
| ------ | --------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **−1** | Debunked                    | The framework's core claims have been contradicted by convergent evidence or decisive rational analysis. The phenomenon it claims to explain either does not exist, or operates in a fundamentally different way. |
| **0**  | Unsupported / Unverifiable  | The claim lacks meaningful evidentiary support or cannot be tested in a way that allows confirmation or disconfirmation.                                                                                          |
| **+1** | Emerging / Speculative      | Some initial evidence or logical support exists, but the claim remains exploratory. Testable, but results are preliminary, inconsistent, or insufficient for confident classification.                            |
| **+2** | Developing Theory           | Meaningful evidentiary support exists. The phenomenon is plausibly real and the explanatory framework is being refined. Results are becoming more consistent, but confirmation is incomplete.                     |
| **+3** | Proven Phenomenon           | The existence of the phenomenon is supported by convergent evidence from multiple independent lines of investigation. The phenomenon is reliably real within its domain. Phenomenon Closure achieved.             |
| **+4** | Complete Mechanistic Theory | Not only is the phenomenon established, but the causal mechanisms producing it are sufficiently understood to generate reliable prediction within the defined domain. Mechanistic Closure achieved.               |


**Important distinctions:**

- ETS evaluates evidentiary standing, not popularity, rhetorical appeal, or cultural influence
- A theory may be culturally dominant and still be ETS 0 or ETS −1
- A theory may be culturally marginal and still be ETS +4
- ETS is the only scale that determines whether a theory is "true" — SES and EIS are diagnostic only

---

#### The Scientific Engagement Scale (SES) — Secondary Scale

**What it measures:** How a theory behaves in relation to scientific inquiry.

**Central question:** *Does this theory participate in scientific investigation?*

**Evaluation criteria:**

- Does it generate testable predictions?
- Does it permit falsification — can it in principle be proven wrong?
- Does it participate in peer review and research programs?
- Does it respond constructively to contradictory findings?
- Does it revise itself when challenged by evidence?

**SES Scale Levels:**


| Score  | Label                                   | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                  |
| ------ | --------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **−2** | Non-Scientific / Structurally Resistant | The framework actively resists scientific evaluation. It is insulated from testing, rejects falsification as a standard, or is explicitly organized around protecting its claims from scrutiny.                              |
| **−1** | Minimal Engagement                      | The framework nominally acknowledges scientific standards but does not meaningfully participate in them. It may selectively use scientific language without subjecting its claims to rigorous testing.                       |
| **0**  | Standalone / No Engagement              | The framework does not engage with scientific methodology. It makes no attempt to generate testable predictions or participate in structured evaluation. Not inherently resistant — simply outside scientific participation. |
| **+1** | Partial Scientific Engagement           | The framework generates some testable claims and participates partially in scientific methodology. Evidence is sought, but methodological rigor is incomplete, inconsistent, or narrowly applied.                            |
| **+2** | Strong Scientific Engagement            | The framework actively engages scientific methodology. It generates testable predictions, accepts falsification in principle, participates in peer review, and responds to contradictory evidence with revision.             |
| **+3** | Core Scientific Program                 | The framework functions as a central research platform, generating ongoing scientific investigation, driving experimental design, and contributing to systematic knowledge production across its domain.                     |


**Critical note:** SES does not measure truth. A theory may exhibit SES +3 and still be debunked. Scientific engagement is a measure of methodological posture, not outcome.

---

#### The Explanatory Integration Scale (EIS) — Secondary Scale

**What it measures:** How a theory fits within the broader architecture of established knowledge.

**Central question:** *Does this theory harmonize with what is already known, or does it fracture the knowledge system?*

**Evaluation criteria:**

- Coherence with independently validated knowledge across other domains
- Resolution of cross-domain tensions — does it help resolve apparent contradictions between fields?
- Contribution to systemic understanding — does it advance the overall picture of reality?
- Does it require the rejection of well-established knowledge without providing extraordinary evidence?

**EIS Scale Levels:**


| Score  | Label                              | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                  |
| ------ | ---------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **−2** | Systemically Incompatible Doctrine | The framework fundamentally conflicts with multiple areas of well-established knowledge. Accepting its claims would require rejecting substantial portions of the validated knowledge base without sufficient justification. |
| **−1** | Fragmenting Theory                 | The framework produces tensions or contradictions with established knowledge in specific domains. It does not integrate cleanly but is not categorically incompatible.                                                       |
| **0**  | Standalone Theory / No Integration | The framework operates independently from other established knowledge domains. It neither conflicts with nor contributes to broader integration. Domain-specific without cross-domain implications.                          |
| **+1** | Domain Integrator                  | The framework integrates well within its primary domain and contributes to coherence within that field, though its cross-domain integration remains limited.                                                                 |
| **+2** | Cross-Domain Integrator            | The framework connects and integrates knowledge across multiple independent domains, reducing explanatory fragmentation and contributing to systemic coherence.                                                              |
| **+3** | Architectonic Integrator           | The framework serves as a foundational organizing principle that integrates knowledge across many domains and constitutes a major structural element of the overall knowledge system.                                        |


**Critical note:** EIS does not measure truth. A theory may exhibit strong integration and still be false. Integration describes structural compatibility and explanatory reach, not evidentiary confirmation.[^1]

### Chapter 10: Overall ATLAS Scoring System

#### Composite Score

The composite ATLAS score is calculated by adding all three scale values (ETS + SES + EIS) under standard conditions.

- **Range:** −5 to +10
- **Maximum (+10):** Mechanistic closure (ETS +4) + Core Scientific Program (SES +3) + Architectonic Integrator (EIS +3)
- **Minimum (−5):** Debunked (ETS −1) + Non-Scientific (SES −2) + Systemically Incompatible (EIS −2)

The composite score is **diagnostic, not truth-determining.** ETS alone determines whether a theory is true, unresolved, or debunked.

---

#### Scoring Override Rules

Two structural rules govern situations where standard aggregation would produce misleading results:

**1. Negative Override Rule** (triggered when ETS = −1):

- Positive SES or EIS scores do NOT contribute to the composite score
- Only negative values across all three scales are summed
- Purpose: Prevents debunked theories from acquiring false legitimacy through methodological engagement or integrative appearance
- Interpretation: A score between −1 and −2 indicates a framework that participated in science but failed evidentiary evaluation. A score between −3 and −5 indicates a framework that was structurally non-scientific or epistemically incompatible from the outset.

**2. Closure Override Rule** (triggered when ETS = +4, mechanistic closure):

- The composite score is anchored at **10**, regardless of SES or EIS values
- Purpose: Prevents domain-specific theories from appearing uncertain simply because they have limited cross-domain integration
- Mechanistic completion represents the highest level of explanatory maturity; scope does not reduce certainty

**Formal expression:**


| Condition                                  | Formula                                       |
| ------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------- |
| Standard (ETS ≥ 0, no mechanistic closure) | ATLAS Score = ETS + SES + EIS                 |
| Debunked (ETS = −1)                        | ATLAS Score = ETS + min(SES, 0) + min(EIS, 0) |
| Mechanistic closure achieved               | ATLAS Score = 10                              |


---

#### Scope of Evaluation

ATLAS evaluates five types of frameworks:

1. **Scientific theory** — proposes an explanation and participates in systematic investigation (testable, falsifiable, responsive to revision)
2. **Speculative claim** — proposes an explanation but lacks meaningful evidentiary accessibility
3. **Philosophical claim** — conceptual assertion about the nature of reality, relying on logical reasoning rather than systematic testing
4. **Religious doctrine** — belief-based claim grounded in tradition, authority, or revelation; can still be evaluated against evidence when it makes claims about reality
5. **Debunked theory** — any framework whose core claims have been contradicted by convergent evidence (ETS = −1)

---

#### Claim Type Distinction

Before any evaluation, ATLAS requires identifying whether the claim is **ontological** or **explanatory**:

- **Ontological claim:** "Does this exist?" (e.g., "evolution occurs," "dark matter exists")
- **Explanatory claim:** "Is this model of how it works accurate?" (e.g., "human needs follow a hierarchical dependency structure")

A real phenomenon does not automatically validate any particular explanatory model. ATLAS must not treat confirmation of a phenomenon as confirmation of every structural claim associated with it. When a framework contains both types, they must be evaluated separately.[^1]

Every ATLAS evaluation follows a fixed nine-step process. The presentation structure (what appears first) differs from the evaluation sequence (what is computed first):

**Evaluation Sequence (required order of analysis):**

1. Describe the framework
2. Identify the unit of analysis and specific claim(s)
3. Classify each claim as ontological or explanatory
4. Determine ETS classification
5. Evaluate SES and EIS
6. Calculate the composite score
7. Interpret the result
8. Apply triggered variation rules (Additional Analysis)
9. Construct and present the ATLAS Evaluation Summary (displayed first, generated last)

**ATLAS Evaluation Summary (presented first) must contain, in order:**

1. Framework or Theory Name

9. Ontological Scale(s)

**Hard Constraints (Non-Optional Rules):**

1. ETS must be determined before SES or EIS

7. All classifications must come from defined criteria (Classification Derivation Rule)
8. Claim type must be identified before any scale analysis (Claim Type Identification Rule)[^1]

## All Concepts & Frameworks Introduced

### Core Framework Concepts

**ATLAS (Assessing Theoretical Legitimacy Across Scales):** The overall structured framework for evaluating the epistemic legitimacy of theories across three independent dimensions.[^1]

**Epistemic legitimacy:** The degree to which a theoretical framework merits rational confidence based on its evidentiary standing, methodological posture, and systemic coherence.[^1]

**Established Truth Scale (ETS):** The primary scale in ATLAS. Evaluates whether a theory accurately describes reality, based on convergence of evidence, predictive reliability, reproducibility, and resistance to falsification. Ranges from −1 to +4.[^1]

**Scientific Engagement Scale (SES):** Secondary scale evaluating how a theory behaves in relation to scientific inquiry — whether it generates testable predictions, permits falsification, participates in peer review, and revises itself when challenged. Ranges from −2 to +3.[^1]

**Explanatory Integration Scale (EIS):** Secondary scale evaluating how a theory fits within the broader architecture of established knowledge — whether it harmonizes with or fractures the knowledge system. Ranges from −2 to +3.[^1]

**Composite ATLAS Score:** Sum of ETS + SES + EIS (under standard conditions), ranging from −5 to +10. Diagnostic summary, not truth-determining.[^1]

**Negative Override Rule:** When ETS = −1 (debunked), only negative SES and EIS values count in the composite. Prevents debunked theories from gaining artificial legitimacy through methodological participation or integrative appearance.[^1]

**Closure Override Rule:** When mechanistic closure is achieved (ETS = +4), the composite score is fixed at 10 regardless of SES or EIS values. Prevents domain-specific theories from appearing uncertain due to limited scope.[^1]

**Non-Compensation Rule:** SES and EIS scores can never increase or decrease truth status. A debunked theory remains debunked; an established theory remains established.[^1]

### Ontological and Epistemic Concepts

**Ontological scale:** Levels of organization in reality defined by structural magnitude and containment. Five bands: Nano, Micro, Intermediate, Macro, Meta.[^1]

**Holonic containment:** The property by which each ontological scale contains the one below it without dissolving its coherence. Nano is contained in Micro; Micro in Intermediate, etc. Containment does not imply reduction.[^1]

**Phenomenon closure:** The stage at which the existence of a phenomenon has been decisively established within its ontological scale.[^1]

**Mechanistic closure:** The stage at which the causal processes producing a phenomenon have been sufficiently mapped to generate reliable prediction within the defined domain.[^1]

**Domain closure:** Explanatory completion within a clearly delimited scope (both phenomenon and mechanistic stability achieved).[^1]

**Scale level closure:** The fundamental organizing processes governing an entire ontological scale are secure.[^1]

**Data expansion layer:** Ongoing refinement, resolution increase, and dataset extension within an already stabilized explanatory framework. Does not imply closure has failed.[^1]

**Epistemic plateau:** The investigative boundary at which further progress within a defined ontological band becomes constrained. Three types: technical (tool-based), structural (physics-based), observational (access-based).[^1]

**Ontological scale closure (civilizational):** The claim that humanity has already identified all major bands of phenomena across which physical reality operates — the complete vertical spectrum from quantum to cosmological. What remains is not discovery of new ontological layers but exploration within known ones.[^1]

**Mechanistic maturity:** A stage in which a scientific domain's governing dynamics are sufficiently understood that most newly discovered phenomena can be interpreted as variations, configurations, or applications of already known mechanisms, rather than requiring entirely new governing principles.[^1]

**Breadth vs. depth of knowledge:** Breadth concerns the fraction of the universe investigated (humanity's is extraordinarily small); depth concerns understanding of the fundamental scales of phenomena (humanity has covered all major levels). Both can be true simultaneously.[^1]

### Philosophical Concepts

**Non-Reductionist Representational Realism:** The philosophical position underlying ATLAS. Affirms that reality exists independently of descriptions, that knowledge operates through representations that can accurately correspond to what is real, and that objective truth is attainable through precise definitions and convergent evidence. Rejects both absolute certainty claims and radical skepticism.[^1]

**Absolute truth:** Reality as it exists independently of description — Kant's *Ding an sich* (thing-in-itself). The territory.[^1]

**Objective truth:** Claims that accurately map the territory using clearly defined symbols and reproducible methods. Expressed through relative language, but refers to real features of the world.[^1]

**Subjective truth:** What a person knows with certainty about their own experience. Real but not externally testable in the same way.[^1]

**Fallibilism:** The philosophical position that knowledge is always provisional and closure is impossible. ATLAS argues this is false — it collapses the distinction between absolute and objective knowledge, is self-contradictory, and undermines the conditions for rational communication.[^1]

**Scientism (as misrepresentation):** The rhetorical framing of science as a closed, dogmatic belief system. ATLAS argues this is a straw man — it targets a constructed version of science rather than the actual method, which is defined by falsifiability, peer review, and reproducibility.[^1]

**Empirical reductionism:** The view that only what can be externally measured counts as real or knowable. ATLAS explicitly rejects this. Science requires claims to be structured for evaluation — not for them to be tangible physical objects.[^1]

**Category collapse:** The conflation of distinct evaluative domains (truth with popularity, correctness with methodological participation, authority with proof). ATLAS's structure is designed to prevent category collapse explicitly.[^1]

**Discriminating wisdom:** The epistemic capacity to recognize that evidence can converge decisively, that closure can be achieved, and that some claims are settled while others remain open. Complementary to epistemic humility.[^1]

**Epistemic humility:** The recognition that knowledge can be refined and models improved. Not equivalent to the claim that no knowledge is ever settled.[^1]

### Claim-Level Concepts

**Ontological claim:** An assertion about whether a phenomenon, process, structure, or type of thing *exists* in reality. Central question: "Is this real?"[^1]

**Explanatory claim:** An assertion about how a real phenomenon is structured, functions, develops, or is best explained. Central question: "Is this model of how it works accurate?"[^1]

**Burden of proof:** Rests on the party making the claim. Asserted claims do not earn credibility through assertion alone.[^1]

**Falsifiability:** A claim's vulnerability to disconfirmation through observation, testing, or comparison with established knowledge. Central criterion for scientific evaluation.[^1]

**Verifiability (scientific observability):** Observable in a way that permits independent verification and systematic study. Requires measurable, repeatable, and independently confirmable evidence.[^1]

## Actionable Takeaways

1. **Apply the three-question test to any claim:** (1) What is its evidentiary status? (2) Does it participate in scientific accountability? (3) Does it cohere with established knowledge? Evaluate these independently.
2. **Lead with ETS in any epistemic evaluation.** Until truth status is determined, secondary dimensions should not be allowed to drive conclusions. A claim cannot be rehabilitated by its methodological elegance alone.
3. **Stop treating the absence of absolute certainty as the presence of genuine uncertainty.** "Scientists don't know everything" is not a valid argument that they know nothing or that competing claims are equivalent. Distinguish refinement from instability.
4. **Match the scale of critique to the scale of the claim.** A molecular biology refinement cannot "overturn" evolutionary theory at the intermediate scale unless it produces actual contradiction at that same level of organization.
5. **Distinguish ontological from explanatory claims before evaluating.** Whether something exists (phenomenon closure) is a different question from whether a particular explanatory model about it is correct.
6. **Recognize the category collapse error when you see it.** When someone argues that scientific consensus is "just institutionalized belief" or that a debunked claim deserves equal time as an "alternative perspective," identify the specific category confusion being made and name it.
7. **Use the composite score diagnostically, not as a ranking.** Two theories with the same composite score may have radically different epistemic statuses. Always check the ETS classification.
8. **Use ATLAS as a writing and communication tool.** When publishing, presenting, or teaching, explicitly stating the ETS/SES/EIS profile of claims helps audiences understand what is established, what is developing, and what remains speculative.
9. **Design AI reasoning systems with explicit epistemic hierarchy.** ATLAS provides a formal architecture for any AI system that needs to evaluate, compare, or communicate the reliability of theoretical claims.[^1]

## Critiques and Counterarguments Raised

The document raises and preemptively addresses several objections:

**"Science is just another perspective / belief system"**
Addressed by the systematic demolition of the "scientism" framing. Science is defined by its method (falsifiability, peer review, reproducibility), not by its conclusions. Calling scientific consensus "belief" ignores the mechanism that produced it.[^1]

**"No knowledge can ever be truly settled (fallibilism)"**
Addressed directly and at length. Fallibilism is diagnosed as a category error (confusing absolute with objective knowledge), self-refuting (it asserts certainty about the limits of certainty), and practically destructive (it dissolves the conditions required for meaningful communication).[^1]

**"Ongoing scientific refinement means nothing is ever truly established"**
Addressed through the concept of the Data Expansion Layer and the Refinement-Closure Distinction. Refinement increases resolution within a stabilized framework; it does not imply the framework is unstable. New species discovered does not reopen the question of whether natural selection governs biological change.[^1]

**"Science is limited to objective, external, measurable phenomena — subjective and intersubjective experience is outside its scope"**
Rejected as empirical reductionism — a misrepresentation of science. Science requires claims to be structured for evaluation; it applies to subjective phenomena (psychology, cognitive science) and intersubjective phenomena (sociology, linguistics) through appropriate methodological adaptations.[^1]

**"The burden of scientific proof unfairly excludes legitimate inquiry"**
Addressed by distinguishing investigable possibilities (open questions) from unsupported assertions and falsified claims. The burden of proof principle protects inquiry from being overwhelmed by untestable proposals, but does not prevent legitimate investigation of any testable claim.[^1]

**"Individual scientists are biased, so science itself is biased"**
A category error: science is not defined by the behavior of individual scientists. It is defined by the method that corrects for those behaviors over time. Personal bias is precisely the problem science is designed to solve.[^1]

**"ATLAS is itself a form of dogmatism — it claims to know what is settled"**
The framework anticipates this by grounding all evaluations in explicit, transparent, reproducible criteria derived from evidence. ATLAS classifications are open to revision if same-scale contradictory evidence emerges. The framework explicitly builds in openness to refinement while affirming that this does not require treating all claims as equally unresolved.[^1]
